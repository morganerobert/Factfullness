# Clustering Analysis – Global Development

## Overview
This analysis explores whether global development can be meaningfully grouped into a small number of clusters using data-driven methods (e.g., K-means and the Elbow Rule).

---

## Research Questions & Answers

### 1. Can the world be meaningfully divided into only two clusters (developed vs developing)?
**Answer:** No  

A two-cluster model is too simplistic and does not capture the diversity and complexity of global development patterns.

---

### 2. How many clusters are suggested by the data (Elbow Rule)?
**Answer:** 6 clusters  

The Elbow Rule indicates that the optimal number of clusters is approximately 6, suggesting a more nuanced structure.

---

### 3. How much of the data structure is explained by 2 clusters vs the optimal number?

| Number of Clusters | Variance Explained |
|-------------------|------------------|
| 2 clusters        | 35.9%            |
| 6 clusters        | 76.7%            |

A 2-cluster model captures less than half of the structure explained by 6 clusters.

---

### 4. Does the evidence support the claim in *Factfulness* that the world can be divided into 4 groups?
**Answer:** Not fully  

While the claim improves on the outdated binary view, the data suggests that:
- More than 4 clusters are needed
- Around 6 clusters better represent global diversity  

---

## Key Takeaways

- The "developed vs developing" dichotomy is too reductive
- Global development follows a multi-dimensional spectrum
- Data-driven clustering reveals greater complexity than simplified models

---

## Conclusion

The world cannot be accurately divided into only two or even four groups.  
A model with approximately 6 clusters provides a significantly better representation of global development patterns.
